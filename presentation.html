GeoChron × Texas PMIS

Adapting a Storyline visualization paper to real pavement data

What the paper proposed, what we kept, what we had to change, and why — a walkthrough of the cohort-based pavement trajectory pipeline.

1 · The paper's core idea

GeoChron's Storyline framework

Paper

Treat each spatiotemporal (ST) series (an entity over time) as a "character," and a group of series moving together as a Storyline session — the same analogy classic Storyline visualizations use for characters interacting in a narrative.

Paper

Pipeline per time slice: (1) split time into slices, (2) correlate every pair of series within a slice, (3) intersect correlation with spatial proximity to build a relation network, (4) run Louvain community detection on that network — each community = one session, (5) lay sessions out with a StoryFlow-style Storyline layout, (6) drill down with an EvoLens lens showing per-entity trends and a normalized "trend motif."

2 · Our adaptation target

Texas PMIS pavement condition data

The paper's data

  • Dense, regularly-sampled ST series
  • Clean single-day time slices
  • Coordinates available for every entity

Our data (PMIS)

  • ~14,810 half-mile road segments, 1996–2024
  • Annual condition scores, sparse and gappy — no daily sampling
  • begin_dfo/end_dfo coordinates empty for our data

Same underlying idea (series that move together over time), a very different data reality — this drove every adaptation decision that follows.

3 · Adaptation: time slice → sliding window

Step 4: multi-year sliding windows replace single-day slices

Paper

A time slice is a single day; a time window wraps three consecutive slices for cross-slice smoothing.

Ours

Annual sampling can't support a single-day slice. We use one W=5-year sliding window, stepped by 1 year (1996–2024 → 25 windows). The window overlap gives us the paper's cross-slice smoothing "for free," so we don't need a separate wrapping step.

Called out in procedure.md as the one non-negotiable adaptation: without it, our sparse annual data has no meaningful "slice" to correlate within.

4 · Adaptation: gaps

Step 3: preserve gaps, never interpolate

Different from a naive port

With sparse annual sampling, an interpolated point can be a large fraction of a segment's signal — enough to manufacture a trend the pavement never had.

Our rule

Leave missing years empty in the section×year matrix. Handle gaps only where they belong — at correlation time (pairwise-complete Pearson, minimum overlap required) — never by fabricating values.

5 · Adaptation: proximity without coordinates

Step 8–9: (roadbed, county) instead of geographic radius

PaperOurs
Same highway within a reference-marker distance, OR same district / within a haversine radius (cross-route corridor effects) Same roadbed AND county — a proxy, since real lat/long isn't populated in this dataset

This is the biggest known simplification in the whole pipeline: it means cohorts can only ever be found within one highway/county pair — a session can never span two different highways, even ones physically close together. Flagged in PROGRESS.md as the first thing to revisit if real coordinates become available.

6 · Straight reuse (no change needed)

What we kept exactly as the paper describes

Kept

Step 6–7 — correlation + thresholding. Pairwise-complete Pearson correlation per window, reduced to a binary yes/no edge at THR=0.7, exactly like the paper's approach (graded weights aren't used for the community-detection decision).

Kept

Step 10 — Louvain community detection. Run on the intersection of correlation and proximity edges. Louvain itself needs no pre-set cluster count, is location-blind, and only produces location-confined communities because cross-location edges don't exist post-filter — identical mechanism to the paper.

Kept

Step 11–12 — session filtering + cross-window tracking. Drop small sessions unless they feed a large neighbor-window session; link sessions window-to-window by largest membership overlap — this produces the Storyline's continuity curves, same as the paper.

7 · Adaptation: normalization, deferred

Step 5: skipped for correlation, used later for EvoLens

Decision

Pearson correlation is provably invariant to shift/scale (z-normalizing a series first doesn't change its Pearson correlation with anything). So Step 5's normalization is mathematically redundant for the correlation math — we skip it there and use raw scores, matching what the paper's underlying math already implies.

Where it's actually used

Normalization (z-score) is applied live, on demand, in the EvoLens trend motif — where the paper genuinely needs it, to compare a session's "shape" independent of absolute level.

8 · Visualization — Level 1

Storyline rendering: window = span, not a point

Early prototype (wrong)

Each window drawn as a single point; single-window memberships rendered as meaningless dots; anchor/interpolation logic produced artificial spikes shooting into empty space.

Current, paper-faithful

Each window is a full-width bar (a span); windows are connected by a thinner Bézier curve. Layout uses per-window barycenter-sweep ordering (6 forward/backward sweeps) with no anchor points at all — directly matching how the paper's Storyline curves pass continuously between session bundles.

9 · Visualization — engineering, not paper-driven

WebGL rendering (a practical addition, not from the paper)

Not in the paper

The paper doesn't specify a rendering technology. Our first D3/SVG implementation became too slow at ~14,810 segments × 25 windows.

Our addition

Hybrid renderer: WebGL for the bulk bar/connector geometry (batched, chunked draw calls to dodge a silent GPU driver limit above ~millions of vertices per call), plus a transparent 2D canvas overlay for text, hover highlighting, and labels.

10 · Visualization — Level 2

EvoLens drill-down: brush-select, not click

Kept, verified against paper.txt

The paper's actual mechanism: brush a rectangle on the Storyline to select a time period + sessions, which pops open a lens with (a) per-entity line charts of the real (un-windowed) trend, and (b) a trend motif — z-score the values within the brushed range, take the lower quartile / median / upper quartile, and shade the quartile band. We initially built this as a click interaction; corrected to brush-select once verified against the paper text.

11 · Display-only fix

Grouping re-keyed to (roadbed, county)

Sessions were already correctly scoped to one (roadbed, county) pair (Step 8/9). But the Storyline's visual bands were originally grouped by roadbed alone — so a highway passing through several counties showed as one mixed band.

Fix

Re-keyed export_storyline_data.py's band grouping to (roadbed, county) tuples: 722 → 1,112 bands, matching the actual constraint sessions were already scoped to. No session/track validity changed — this only fixed the display grouping to match data that was already correct.
